The analysis reveals interesting patterns across different circuit types:

High-Speed Tracks (Monza, Silverstone): All models perform better, with LightGBM achieving MAE below 0.7s. The more predictable nature of these tracks allows models to capture patterns more effectively.

Street Circuits (Monaco, Singapore): More challenging predictions due to unpredictable factors (safety cars, crashes, track position). LightGBM still leads but with slightly higher errors (~0.77s MAE).

Technical Circuits (Mexico, Abu Dhabi): Moderate difficulty. LightGBM maintains consistent advantage with ~0.71s average MAE.

3. Algorithm Comparison

LightGBM: Uses leaf-wise tree growth, which is more efficient and often leads to better accuracy. Handles missing values and categorical features natively. Best overall performance.

XG Boost & CART: Both use level-wise tree growth. Show identical performance as they share the same underlying algorithm architecture. Good baseline but consistently outperformed by LightGBM.

Random Forest: Parallel tree construction provides feature importance insights but struggles with feature interactions compared to boosting methods. Slightly worse performance overall.

4. Performance Metrics Analysis

Mean Absolute Error (MAE): LightGBM's 0.708s average means predictions are typically within 0.7 seconds of actual race times - excellent for F1 where margins are often razor-thin.

Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE): At 0.979s, LightGBM shows better handling of outliers and extreme predictions compared to other models (1.016s+).

R¬≤ Score: While all models show negative R¬≤ (indicating room for improvement), LightGBM's -0.207 is significantly better than competitors' -0.275 to -0.288, showing it captures more variance in the data.
